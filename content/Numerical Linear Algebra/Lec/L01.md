## Basic Matrix Algebra

### 1. Vectors and Vector Spaces

> [!def] Vector
> A vector is an element with both magnitude and direction. We represent vectors as columns:
> $$
> \mathbf{v} =
> \begin{bmatrix}
> a \\ b \\ c
> \end{bmatrix}
> \in \mathbb{R}^3.
> $$

> [!def] Vector Space (over $\mathbb{R}$)
> A set $V$ is a vector space if it is closed under vector addition and scalar multiplication and contains the zero vector:
> - If $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in V$, then $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \in V$.
> - If $\mathbf{v} \in V$ and $c \in \mathbb{R}$, then $c\mathbf{v} \in V$.
> - There exists $\mathbf{0} \in V$ such that $\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{v}$ for all $\mathbf{v} \in V$.
> Additional (standard) axioms include associativity, commutativity of addition, and existence of additive inverses.

### 2. Vector Operations

> [!def] Dot Product (Inner Product in $\mathbb{R}^n$)
> For $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$,
> $$
> \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} \;=\; \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i \;\in\; \mathbb{R}.
> $$
> Example (in $\mathbb{R}^3$):
> $$
> [u_1,u_2,u_3]
> \begin{bmatrix}
> v_1\\ v_2\\ v_3
> \end{bmatrix}
> = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + u_3v_3.
> $$
> The dot product produces a scalar.

> [!def] Vector Norm (Euclidean 2-Norm)
> The length (magnitude) of $\mathbf{u}$ is
> $$
> \|\mathbf{u}\| \;=\; \sqrt{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{u}} \;=\; \sqrt{\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{u}}.
> $$

> [!def] Angle Between Vectors
> For nonzero $\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$,
> $$
> \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w} \;=\; \|\mathbf{v}\|\,\|\mathbf{w}\|\,\cos\theta
> \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
> \cos\theta \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|\,\|\mathbf{w}\|}.
> $$
> The angle $\theta$ is well-defined when both vectors are nonzero.

> [!cor] Orthogonality Criterion
> Vectors $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$ are orthogonal (denoted $\mathbf{v} \perp \mathbf{w}$) if and only if $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} = 0$ (equivalently, $\cos\theta = 0$).

> [!def] Orthonormal Set
> A set $\{\mathbf{q}_1,\dots,\mathbf{q}_k\}$ is orthonormal if
> $$
> \mathbf{q}_i \cdot \mathbf{q}_j = \begin{cases}
> 1, & i=j,\\
> 0, & i\ne j,
> \end{cases}
> \quad\text{equivalently}\quad \|\mathbf{q}_i\|=1 \text{ and } \mathbf{q}_i \perp \mathbf{q}_j \ (i\ne j).
> $$

> [!lem] Expansion of Squared Norm (Parallelogram Identity Component)
> For any $\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$,
> $$
> \|\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\|^2
> = (\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})\cdot(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})
> = \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 + 2\,\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} + \|\mathbf{w}\|^2.
> $$

> [!thm] Triangle Inequality
> For all $\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$,
> $$
> \|\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\| \;\le\; \|\mathbf{v}\| + \|\mathbf{w}\|.
> $$
> 
> > [!pf] Proof
> > By the lemma,
> > $$
> > \|\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\|^2
> > = \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 + 2\,\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} + \|\mathbf{w}\|^2
> > \le \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 + 2\,\|\mathbf{v}\|\,\|\mathbf{w}\| + \|\mathbf{w}\|^2
> > = (\|\mathbf{v}\|+\|\mathbf{w}\|)^2,
> > $$
> > where the inequality uses Cauchy–Schwarz: $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} \le \|\mathbf{v}\|\,\|\mathbf{w}\|$. Taking square roots yields the claim.

### 3. Matrices and Linear Combinations

> [!def] Matrix–Vector Multiplication as a Linear Combination of Columns
> Let $A = [\mathbf{a}_1\, \mathbf{a}_2\, \cdots\, \mathbf{a}_n] \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ and $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Then
> $$
> A\mathbf{x} =
> \begin{bmatrix}
> \mathbf{a}_1 & \mathbf{a}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{a}_n
> \end{bmatrix}
> \begin{bmatrix}
> x_1\\ x_2\\ \vdots\\ x_n
> \end{bmatrix}
> \;=\;
> x_1\mathbf{a}_1 + x_2\mathbf{a}_2 + \cdots + x_n\mathbf{a}_n.
> $$
> Example:
> $$
> \begin{bmatrix}
> 2 & 5\\
> 3 & 7
> \end{bmatrix}
> \begin{bmatrix}
> 1\\[2pt] 1
> \end{bmatrix}
> =
> \begin{bmatrix}
> 2\cdot 1 + 5\cdot 1\\
> 3\cdot 1 + 7\cdot 1
> \end{bmatrix}
> =
> \begin{bmatrix}
> 7\\ 10
> \end{bmatrix}.
> $$

> [!def] Column Space
> The column space of $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ is
> $$
> \operatorname{Col}(A) \;=\; \{A\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^n\}
> \;=\; \operatorname{span}\{\mathbf{a}_1,\dots,\mathbf{a}_n\} \;\subseteq\; \mathbb{R}^m.
> $$

### 4. Linear Independence and Rank

> [!def] Linear Independence of Columns
> The columns of $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ are linearly independent if
> $$
> A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}.
> $$
> Equivalently, no column is a linear combination of the others.

> [!thm] Linear Dependence When n > m
> If $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ with $n>m$, then the columns of $A$ are linearly dependent.
> 
> > [!pf] Proof (Dimension Argument)
> > The column space $\operatorname{Col}(A)$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$, hence has dimension at most $m$. Therefore $A$ cannot have more than $m$ linearly independent columns. If $n>m$, at least one column must be a linear combination of the others.

> [!case] Example: 2×3 Matrix
> Let
> $$
> A =
> \begin{bmatrix}
> a & b & c\\
> d & e & f
> \end{bmatrix}
> \in \mathbb{R}^{2\times 3}.
> $$
> The three columns lie in $\mathbb{R}^2$. By the theorem, at most two can be independent; thus the columns are necessarily linearly dependent.

> [!def] Rank
> The rank of $A$, denoted $\operatorname{rank}(A)$, is
> $$
> \operatorname{rank}(A) \;=\; \dim\big(\operatorname{Col}(A)\big) \;=\; \dim\big(\operatorname{Row}(A)\big).
> $$
> Consequences:
> - The number of independent columns equals the number of independent rows (column rank = row rank).
> - Columns are independent if and only if $\operatorname{rank}(A) = n$ (full column rank).
> - Rows are independent if and only if $\operatorname{rank}(A) = m$ (full row rank).
