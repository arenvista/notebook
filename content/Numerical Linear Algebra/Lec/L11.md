# Full QR Factorization

> [!pf] Full QR Factorization
>
> $$
> 	\begin{gathered}
> 		\underbrace{A \in  \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}}_{\text{Tall and Skinny }  (m > n)}  =
> 		\underbrace{
> 			\begin{bmatrix}
> 				a & a_{1,2} & \dots  & a_{1,n} \\
> 				a & \ddots  &                  \\
> 				a &         & \ddots           \\
> 				a &         &        & a_{m,n} \\
> \end{bmatrix}
> 		}_{A_{m \times n}} =
> 		\underbrace{
> 			\begin{bmatrix}
> 				q & q_{1,2} & \dots  & q_{1,m} \\
> 				q & \ddots  &                  \\
> 				q &         & \ddots           \\
> 				q &         &        & q_{m,m} \\
> \end{bmatrix}
> 		}_{Q_{m \times m}}
> 		\underbrace{
> 			\begin{bmatrix}
> 				r & r_{1,2} & \dots  & r_{1,n} \\
> 				r & \ddots  &                  \\
> 				r &         & \ddots           \\
> 				r &         &        & r_{m,n} \\
> \end{bmatrix}
> 		}_{R_{m \times n}} \\
> 		A = QR =
> 		\begin{bmatrix}
> 			\hat{Q} & Q
> \end{bmatrix}
> 		\begin{bmatrix}
> 			\hat{R} & 0
> \end{bmatrix} = \hat{Q}\hat{R} + Q \cdot 0
> 	\end{gathered}
> $$
>
> > [!cor] Implication of Subspace Relationships
> > Note that the columns of $\hat{Q}$ form the basis for $Row(A)$
> >
> > - The columns of $q_j$ for $j>n$ are orthogonal to $Range(A)$. Assuming that $A$ is of full rank and they consitute the basis of $Nul(A^T)$

> [!lem] Eigenvalue/Eigenvector Uniqueness
> $$ A \underbrace{\vec{x}}_{Eigenvector} = \underbrace{\lambda}_{Eigenvalue} \vec{x}$$
> Note that eigenvalues are unique for each eigenvector, but eigenvectors are not unqiue as they are used to describe an eigen space.

> [!def] Eigenvalue Decomposition
> Ideally, we can write
> $$
> 	\begin{gathered}
> 		X = [\vec{x_1}, \vec{x_2}, \dots, \vec{x_n} ] \quad
> 		\Lambda  =
> 		\begin{bmatrix}
> 			\lambda_1 &           &        &           \\
> 			          & \lambda_2 &        &           \\
> 			          &           & \ddots &           \\
> 			          &           &        & \lambda_n \\
> 		\end{bmatrix} \\ \\
> 		|\{ \lambda_1, \lambda_2, \dots, \lambda_n  \}| = Rank(A) \iff AX = X\Lambda \iff A = X \Lambda X^{-1} \iff \text{$A$ is diagonalizable} \\
> 		\text{In which we can say that $A$ is \textbf{similar} to a diagonal matrix}
> 	\end{gathered}
> $$
>
> > [!def] Similar Matricies
> > $A,B$ are said to be similar if there exists invertible matrix $M$ such that:
> > $$ A = MBM^{-1} $$ 

> [!cor] Single Positive Definite Matricies
> Note for Single Positive Definite (SPD) Matricies:
> We can find the decomposition and in fact we may write: 
> $$ \underbrace{A = \sum_{n=1} \lambda_i q_i q_i^T}_{\text{Sum of Rank One Matricies}} $$ 

> [!def] Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
> Note Eigenvalue Decomposition works only if $A$ is a square matrix. In cases where $A^{m \times n}, ~m \neq n$ we can apply SVD.
> $$A = U \Sigma V^T$$
